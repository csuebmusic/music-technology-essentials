# Project 1 sample bank: preparation notes

**Location:** `/music/shared/sample-banks/project1/` on the NAS
**Curated by:** Inés

---

## Why this matters

Project 1 is built entirely from the sounds in this folder. The quality, variety, and conceptual coherence of the bank determines how much creative latitude students actually have. Too few sounds → everyone's piece sounds the same. Too many → students drown in choice and never start. Wrong kinds → students can't make anything interesting.

This is one of the most important pieces of pre-semester prep.

---

## Curatorial direction

The bank should have **enough variety to make distinct pieces** without being overwhelming. The aesthetic frame is **musique concrète–style found sounds**: recognizable everyday material that students can recontextualize, manipulate, and arrange.

A pedagogically important secondary frame: students should be able to hear sounds through the lens of their **envelope**: attack, sustain, release. The bank should include a mix that surfaces this vocabulary naturally.

### Envelope variety to aim for

Roughly balanced across these categories; students should be able to find sounds in each:

- **Sharp attack, no sustain, fast release**: clicks, snaps, claps, drops, hits, taps, quick percussive sounds
- **Sharp attack, short sustain, fast release**: single notes plucked or struck, short voiced syllables, single bird calls
- **Soft attack, long sustain, slow release**: bowed strings, sustained voice, drones, hums
- **Continuous / "infinite" sustain**: running water, rain, traffic, wind, machinery, refrigerator hum
- **Sounds with complex envelopes**: door closing (attack-decay-attack), a sentence spoken (multiple onsets), a glass filling (rising sustain)

This isn't a checklist students need to be aware of; it's a curatorial principle. When the lecture on Mon Wk 3 introduces the envelope concept, students should be able to *immediately* find sounds in the bank that exemplify each shape.

### Suggested rough scope

- **40–80 sounds total**: enough variety, not overwhelming
- **Mix of short (under 5 sec) and longer (5–30 sec) sources**: students need both for editing flexibility
- **Mostly 44.1 kHz / 16-bit WAV**: matches what they'll export for Project 1
- **Mostly mono or true stereo**: avoid sounds with weird stereo treatment that students will have to undo

---

## What NOT to include

- Copyrighted music or recognizable musical phrases; keep the source material as "found sounds," not borrowed compositions
- Loops or tempo-locked material; students should be hearing source sounds, not pre-made patterns
- Anything offensive, alarming, or distressing without a clear pedagogical reason
- Voice recordings of identifiable individuals without their consent
- Anything so long it'll become *the* piece if a student just imports it

## Folder structure

```
/music/shared/sample-banks/project1/
  attack-sharp/
  attack-soft/
  sustain-long/
  sustain-short/
  texture-continuous/
  voice-and-language/
  found-objects/
  natural-environment/
  mechanical-electronic/
  README.txt   <- short note about what's in the bank, license terms
```

The categories above are *suggested*; adjust as needed based on what you actually curate. The point is that students browsing the bank can find sounds by character (envelope) or by source type. Both views are useful.

A `README.txt` in the bank itself, written for students, explains:
- That all sounds are free to use for Project 1
- Provenance (your recordings, public-domain archives, Freesound CC0 material, etc.)
- How to credit if relevant
- A reminder of the project rules (only sounds from this folder, etc.)

## NAS access

The bank lives at `/music/shared/sample-banks/project1/`. This is read-only for students; they can browse and copy from it but can't modify it. This needs to be set when the NAS is configured (talk to IT or whoever set up the share).

When students need to use a sound, they'll **copy** it from the shared bank into their own `~/Documents/lastname/project-01/sources/` folder. This way the master bank stays clean and students have local copies they can work with destructively.

---

## Maintenance across semesters

The bank can be reused or refreshed each semester. Worth keeping a "core" set that works well and rotating in new material as you find it. If you swap material between semesters, save the previous version in an archive folder in case someone wants to compare.
