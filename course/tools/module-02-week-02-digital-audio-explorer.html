Sines, sums, the harmonic series, and timbre: by ear and by hand.

Monday's reading introduced waveforms, frequency, and amplitude as abstract ideas. This lab takes those ideas and lets you hear them. Each tool below adds one new piece of the picture: from a single pure tone, to two or three tones at once, to the harmonic series that builds a "note," to the recipe of partial amplitudes that gives every instrument its specific sound. Keep your headphones at a moderate volume the whole way through. Sine tones are loud and fatiguing. Start quiet, listen briefly, stop when you're done.

1 · A single sine

A sine wave is the simplest possible sound: a single frequency, one smooth oscillation, nothing else. It almost never occurs in the natural world: voices, instruments, traffic, water, all of it has a more complicated shape than this. But sine waves are the atoms out of which every other sound is built. Before we sum them, we need to hear one on its own.

The tool below lets you set the frequency (how fast it wiggles, heard as pitch) and the amplitude (how big the wiggle is, heard as loudness). Press play, then move the sliders. The waveform updates in real time so you can see what you're hearing.

2 · Summing sines

Real sounds aren't sines. A struck piano string, a sung vowel, a bowed cello, a barking dog. None of these has the smooth, pure shape you just heard. But here's the surprising thing: every sound, no matter how complex, can be built by adding sine waves together. This is one of the deepest results in the science of sound, and it's the reason we spent time on sine waves in the first place. Sines are the atoms.

The tool below lets you sum up to six sine waves at once. Each one has its own frequency and its own amplitude. The big curve at the top is the actual waveform you're hearing: the sum of whatever sines you've turned up.

Two terms to know going forward:

  • The fundamental is the lowest sine in a complex sound. It's usually what you'd identify as the "pitch" of the sound.
  • A partial is any one sine in the mix, including the fundamental. A complex tone has a fundamental and some number of additional partials.

3 · The harmonic series

You just heard that arbitrary sums of sines don't sound like a coherent note. They sound like a stack of separate tones, or like noise. So how does any real instrument (which is a sum of sines, remember) manage to sound like a single, unified pitch?

The answer is the harmonic series. When the partials of a sound sit at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency (1×, 2×, 3×, 4×, 5×, 6×, ...), your ear fuses them together and hears a single note at the fundamental's pitch. The higher partials don't get heard as separate notes; they become part of the character of that one note.

The tool below has the same six partials as before, but the frequencies are now locked to harmonic ratios of a fundamental you choose. You only control the amplitudes. Press play with all amplitudes at zero, then turn them up one at a time and listen.

4 · Timbre

You just heard that the harmonic series turns six different frequencies into one perceived note. But here's the next question: if a flute and a clarinet play the same note (same fundamental, same harmonic ratios), why do they sound completely different?

The answer is timbre: the unique sonic fingerprint of a sound, separate from its pitch and its loudness. Timbre is what lets you tell a violin from a trumpet without seeing them. And the largest single contributor to an instrument's timbre is the recipe of partial amplitudes. Two sounds with the same fundamental and the same harmonic ratios but different relative amplitudes for each partial will sound like two different instruments.

One thing to know before we dig in: we've been working with six partials in the previous tools because that was enough to make the fusion of the harmonic series easy to hear. Real instruments are far richer. A bowed violin tone can have thirty or more audible partials; a trumpet, a clarinet, a singing voice, all stack many more sines than we've been using. The tool below opens the count up to twelve, which is still less than reality but enough to make timbral character much more vivid.

The tool starts with a default mix of all twelve partials at decreasing amplitudes. Press play and listen, then move the amplitude sliders around. Hold onto what the default sounds like at the start: by the end of this section you'll find out it's a recipe for one of the most common timbres in synthesis.

Three classic recipes

Now do something more focused. Below are three specific recipes: target amplitudes for each of the twelve partials. Press Reset first to return to the default, then dial in the new values one slider at a time. After each recipe is set, listen carefully and look at the live waveform display. The shape that appears in the live display should match the small reference shape next to the recipe.

What you build with these recipes isn't arbitrary. Each one is one of the fundamental waveforms of synthesis: the basic oscillator types built into nearly every synthesizer ever made, including the synths in Ableton you'll meet in Module 4. The math behind why specific patterns of partial amplitudes produce these specific shapes was worked out in the early 1800s by Joseph Fourier; it's how we know that any periodic waveform can be decomposed into a sum of sines, and how synthesists know exactly which sines to add to get a target sound.

As you set the sliders, watch the live waveform display: when your recipe is right, the shape on screen should be a recognizable version of the small reference shape next to the recipe. The match won't be perfect: twelve partials can only approximate the ideal infinitely-rich shape, and you'll see the corners of the square slightly rounded and the sawtooth's drop slightly wavy. That residual ripple is called the Gibbs phenomenon and it's a real feature of building shapes from a finite stack of sines. The takeaway: even with our limited ingredients, a clear recipe produces a recognizable result. This is how synthesizers work, and you're working in the same dimension that orchestrators, synthesists, and sound designers spend their whole careers in.

What you've heard

Four ideas from one chain of demos:

  1. Sines are atoms. The simplest sound is a single sine: one frequency, one amplitude. Almost nothing in the world sounds like this, but everything in the world can be built from this.
  2. Summing arbitrary sines doesn't make a note. Pile up sines at random frequencies and you get something that sounds like noise, or like several separate tones, never like a unified pitched sound.
  3. The harmonic series is what makes a note. When partials line up at integer multiples of a fundamental, the ear fuses them. Six different frequencies become one note.
  4. Timbre is the recipe. Same harmonic ratios, different amplitudes, different sound. A clarinet, a flute, a vocal "ahhh," a trumpet: all are recipes of partial amplitudes over a shared harmonic-series scaffold.

You'll hear partials and timbre referenced throughout the rest of the course. When you start mixing in Module 2, the EQ knobs you'll use are tools for selectively boosting or cutting partials. When you reach synthesis in Module 4, building a sound by adding sines is one of the most direct synthesis techniques (it's called additive synthesis) and it works exactly the way these tools work.

Vocabulary

Sine wave
The simplest possible waveform: a single frequency oscillating smoothly. The atom of sound.
Pure tone
A sound consisting of a single sine wave. Rare in the world; common in test signals and as a building block.
Complex tone
A sound made of multiple sine waves added together. Almost every real-world musical sound is a complex tone.
Fundamental
The lowest partial in a complex tone. Usually what your ear hears as "the pitch" of the sound.
Partial
Any single sine wave in a complex tone, including the fundamental. A trumpet note has a fundamental and a stack of partials above it.
Harmonic series
The set of partials at integer multiples of a fundamental: 1×, 2×, 3×, 4×, ... When partials follow this pattern, the ear fuses them into a single perceived note.
Harmonic
A partial that sits at an integer multiple of the fundamental. (Some non-musical sounds, like bells, gongs, and drums, have inharmonic partials, which is why they don't have a clear pitch.)
Timbre
The character of a sound that lets you distinguish two notes of the same pitch and loudness. Largely determined by the relative amplitudes of the partials.
Additive synthesis
Building sounds by literally adding sine waves together. The technique these tools use, and a real synthesis approach you'll encounter in Module 4.
