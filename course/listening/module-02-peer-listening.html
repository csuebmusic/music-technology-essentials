Peer Listening: Project 1

Listen to your classmates' Project 1 pieces and respond to a few that struck you.

After Wednesday's submission, the class listening folder fills up with everyone's Project 1 pieces. This assignment asks you to listen to all of them and write briefly about three or four that stood out to you. The point isn't to evaluate; it's to listen carefully to peers' first pieces and notice what choices they made. We'll discuss the pieces briefly at the start of class on Mon Sep 21, so come prepared.

How to listen

The class listening folder is at /music/shared/mus-381-fall-2026/project-01-pieces/. Mount the NAS and open the folder. You'll see ~25 files, one per classmate.

Listen on headphones if possible. Listen all the way through each piece, even when something doesn't grab you immediately. Most of these pieces are 90 seconds to 2 minutes, so the full folder is roughly 45 minutes of listening, about the time of a movie's first act. Set aside a quiet block to do it.

Don't multitask while listening. These are your peers' first pieces; they put real time into them.

What to write

Pick three or four pieces that stood out to you, for whatever reason. They might be pieces that surprised you, that did something you wouldn't have thought of, that you wish you'd made, or that simply held your attention in a way you noticed. They don't have to be the "best" pieces, just the ones you found interesting.

For each piece you write about, give us:

  1. Who made it (just the filename or last name).
  2. One specific thing you noticed. A sound choice, a structural decision, a transition, a moment of contrast, a use of silence, a particular edit. Be specific. Not "I liked it" but "the sudden cut at around 0:40 changed the whole feel of the piece."
  3. One question you'd ask the maker. What are you curious about? "How did you decide to put the metallic sounds at the end instead of the beginning?" or "Was the long silence at the middle intentional, and how did you choose its length?" Real questions, not rhetorical.

About 50 to 80 words per piece. Concise and specific beats long and vague. You're listening as a peer, not a critic.

Tone

This is a low-stakes assignment, but the way you write about peers' work matters. Be generous and curious. If a piece didn't work for you, write about something else (there's no requirement to critique). The pieces you choose to write about are themselves a form of feedback (the work that holds your attention is the work you're learning from).

The questions you ask will sometimes get answered in the Mon Sep 21 discussion. Bring them.

lastname-peer-listening-01.docx (lowercase, hyphens, your last name).
Length
About 200 to 320 words total: 50-80 words for each of 3 or 4 pieces. Concise is fine; don't pad.
Formatting
12 pt, double-spaced, 1-inch margins. Use Times New Roman, Garamond, or another standard serif. Include your name, the course (MUS 381), the assignment, and the date at the top.
Citations
Not required for this assignment, since you're writing about classmates' work, not external sources. Just identify each piece by classmate's last name or filename.
A note on giving and receiving feedback

What you write about a classmate's piece may end up in front of them, in some form, eventually. Even if it doesn't, the way you write about others' work is the practice that builds you into someone whose feedback others want. Specific is better than vague. Curious is better than judgmental. The point is to listen well, not to score.
