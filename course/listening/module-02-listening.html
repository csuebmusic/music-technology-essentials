Listening: Musique concrète

Due Mon Sep 14, 2026 · Submit on Canvas

Project 1 asks you to build a piece from existing sound material — to treat sound as something you cut, shape, and arrange, rather than something you perform live or notate on a staff. This is the central idea of musique concrète, a tradition started in Paris in 1948 by Pierre Schaeffer. He recorded everyday sounds — trains, doors, voices, water — and worked with them as raw material, the way a sculptor works with stone.

This week's listening introduces you to the tradition. Listen with the ears of someone who is about to make a piece this way. You'll be asked to think about what techniques you hear, what choices the composers made, and what you might try yourself.

Listen to all three

1 — Pierre Schaeffer · Étude aux chemins de fer (1948)

The first piece of musique concrète ever made. Built entirely from train sounds. About 3 minutes. Listen on Spotify, YouTube, or any streaming service — search "Schaeffer Étude aux chemins de fer."

2 — Pierre Henry · Variations pour une porte et un soupir, opening section (1963)

Built from the sound of a single door and a single sigh. The full work is long — listen to just the first 5 minutes of the opening movement. Search "Henry Variations pour une porte et un soupir."

3 — A contemporary example — your choice

Find one piece made in the last 20 years that you'd describe as continuing the musique concrète tradition — sound-collage, found-sound composition, or sample-based work that treats source material the way Schaeffer and Henry did. A few possible directions to search if you're stuck: Christian Marclay, Matmos, Holly Herndon, Oneohtrix Point Never, Yasunao Tone, or a hip-hop producer whose work is built on samples.

Note what you chose and why — that's part of your response.

Questions

Answer all four. Half a page total — concise is fine. Submit on Canvas as a text response.

  1. What do you hear? Describe the source sounds in one of the historical pieces (Schaeffer or Henry). What are they actually? How recognizable are they?
  2. How is it shaped? Pick one of the historical pieces and describe how it's structured over time. Does it build? Repeat? Vary? What makes it cohere as a piece rather than a random collection of sounds?
  3. What technique stands out? Across the three pieces, identify one specific compositional or editing technique you noticed — repetition, layering, sudden silence, pitch-shifting, stuttering, etc. — and describe how it's used.
  4. What will you try? Based on this listening, what's one specific thing you want to try in your own Project 1? Be concrete — not "I want to make something interesting" but "I want to try repeating a single sound at different speeds" or similar.

A note on listening

Use headphones if you can. Listen all the way through at least once before answering. Most musique concrète is not background music — it asks for your attention. If a piece feels strange or boring at first, sit with it. The tradition rewards patient listening.

About these listening assignments

This is the first of several listening assignments across the semester. The questions follow a consistent pattern: description, analysis, and what you'd try in your own work. Listen, write what you actually hear, and don't worry about being wrong.
