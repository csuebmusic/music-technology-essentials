Project 1: Musique concrète

A short piece built from a curated sample bank, using the editing vocabulary from Module 02.

Build a 2-minute piece entirely from source sounds provided on the NAS. The piece should demonstrate the editing and mixing skills you've learned in Module 2. This is your first piece in the course: the point is not to make a polished commercial track, it's to use the techniques you've learned to make something with intention, where every cut, fade, and mix decision is yours. There is no stylistic requirement beyond "use the source sounds creatively": your piece can be rhythmic, abstract, narrative, atmospheric, or anything else.

Constraints

  • Length: 2 minutes maximum (90 seconds minimum)
  • Source material: Only sounds from the project sample bank at /music/shared/sample-banks/project1/. You may use any sound from the bank, in any combination, processed in any way.
  • Software: Audacity
  • Format: Final export as WAV, 44.1 kHz, 16-bit (we'll cover what those mean)
  • Filename: lastname-project01.wav (see Submission below for full file flow)

What "demonstrating skills" means

A successful piece will show that you:

  • Listened first: chose source sounds with intention rather than randomly
  • Edited deliberately: cuts, fades, and time placements are choices, not accidents
  • Built structure: the piece has shape over time, it goes somewhere, it isn't 2 minutes of one texture (see On structure below)
  • Mixed thoughtfully: levels are balanced, frequencies have room to breathe, stereo placement is considered
  • Used the tools you've learned: the techniques covered in Module 2 are visible in the piece

You don't need to use every technique. A piece that masterfully uses three editing techniques is stronger than one that crams in every effect Audacity offers.

On structure

For most of you this will be the first time you've thought about form, the overall shape of a piece across its full duration. Form is one of the things that turns a sequence of sounds into a piece of music. It's a deliberate decision, not an accident.

The simplest way in: think of your piece as a small set of sections, each with its own character. A section might be defined by a particular sound, a particular texture, a particular density, or a particular emotional quality. Sections give a piece a sense of going somewhere.

One classical shape, useful as a starting point, is ABA:

Imagine your sounds fall into two groups. Section A might be built from sharp metallic hits arranged sparsely. Section B might be built from sustained water textures, dense and continuous. The return to A might bring back the metallic hits but layered with fragments of the water texture, so the listener hears both worlds together. That's a complete piece in three sections.

ABA is one possibility, not the only one. Other shapes you might consider:

  • Through-composed: no return. The piece moves through a sequence of ideas and ends somewhere new. Useful when you want the listener to feel a journey.
  • Narrative arc: a single direction across the whole piece. Quiet to loud, sparse to dense, chaotic to calm, or the reverse. The shape is gradual rather than sectional.
  • Terraced: sections that progressively add or remove elements. Each new section piles more on, or strips more away, than the last.
  • Drone with events: a continuous underlying texture (a single sustained sound, or a slowly shifting one) with discrete events placed on top of it. The drone provides continuity; the events provide change.

Whichever shape you choose, choose it deliberately. Before you finish, you should be able to draw your piece on a napkin: how many sections, what changes between them, where the listener is asked to pay attention to what.

Process expectations

This is a project-based class, and the process matters as much as the product:

  • Start early. First experiments happen in the Wed Wk 3 lab. Don't wait until Wk 5.
  • Save versions. lastname-project01-v1.wav, v2, v3. You'll want to be able to go back.
  • Take breaks. Listen with fresh ears. Most mixing decisions get worse the longer you stare at the screen without resting.
  • Ask for feedback during lab time. The TA and your classmates are resources.

Rubric

Project 1 is graded out of 100 points across five dimensions, equally weighted at 20 points each.

Source material use (20)

  • Sounds chosen with intention, not at random
  • Variety used effectively (or limitation used effectively, if intentional)
  • Source material is recognizable as such, transformed creatively, or somewhere in between (your call, but defensible)

Editing craft (20)

  • Cuts are clean: no clicks, pops, or accidental cutoffs
  • Fades used where needed: no abrupt unintended starts/stops
  • Selections are precise: silence between events is intentional
  • Audacity techniques from Module 2 are visible in the piece

Mixing craft (20)

  • Levels are balanced: nothing is buried, nothing is overpowering
  • Frequencies have space: EQ used to give elements room
  • Stereo placement is considered
  • Dynamics treatment (if used) supports the piece, doesn't crush it

Structure & form (20)

  • The piece has shape: beginning, middle, end (in some form)
  • Time is used intentionally: pacing decisions are visible
  • 2 minutes feels like the right length for what was built (not artificially padded or rushed)

Process & craft (20)

  • Project folder is organized: versions saved, files named per convention
  • Final export is correctly formatted (WAV, 44.1/16, named correctly)
  • Submitted on time, in both the private working folder and the class listening folder
  • Evidence of revision: the final piece is clearly not a first draft

What I'm not grading

  • Style or genre. A noisy abstract piece and a rhythmic structured piece can both score 100.
  • Technical perfection. Module 2 is the first time most of you have done any of this. Effort and attention matter more than polish.
  • Length within the range. A 95-second piece and a 2-minute piece are both fine. Don't pad to hit a number.
  • Whether I personally like the piece. The rubric is the rubric.
