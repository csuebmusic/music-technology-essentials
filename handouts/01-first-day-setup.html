Module 01 · Computer & Studio Fluency

Your First Day

Get set up. Make a sound. Save a file.

Welcome. By the end of class today, you'll have a working folder set up on this computer, a master folder set up on the lab server (the NAS), and your first audio file synced between the two. If anything in this handout looks unfamiliar, that's expected. We'll go through everything together.

What we're doing today

  1. Get comfortable with the Mac and Finder.
  2. Set up your local folder on this computer, and verify your account on the class file server (the NAS).
  3. Plug in your audio interface, microphone, MIDI keyboard, and headphones.
  4. Set your levels and make a short recording: your first piece of work in this class.
  5. Upload your work to the NAS so you can pick it up from any computer next time.
A note about the lab

The room is MB2525, shared between the Music and Art departments. Please leave your station the way you found it. The cables you'll touch are at your station: audio interface, mic, headphones, MIDI keyboard. The computer itself is mounted behind the monitor and you won't need to touch it.

Part 1 · The Mac and Finder

If you've never used a Mac before, this part is for you. If you have, you can use this section as a refresher.

The basics of the screen

  • The bar across the top is the menu bar. The words there change depending on what app you're using.
  • The bar at the bottom (or side) with icons is the Dock. Apps live there.
  • The space behind everything is the Desktop.
Don't save work to the Desktop

The Desktop on the lab computers gets cleared periodically, and anything saved there will be lost. Always save your work to ~/Documents/lastname/: never to the Desktop, the Downloads folder, or anywhere else. We'll set up that lastname folder shortly.

Open Finder

  1. Look at the Dock at the bottom of the screen.
  2. Find the icon that looks like a blue and white smiley face. That's Finder.
  3. Click it once. A window opens. This is how you look at files on the computer.

Make a folder

The next few steps are a quick practice run: we'll make a throwaway folder on the Desktop, drop a file in, and delete it. This is just to learn the mechanics. Your real work goes in your ~/Documents/lastname/ folder, which we'll set up shortly.

  1. In the Finder window, click Desktop in the left sidebar.
  2. From the menu bar at the top, click File, then New Folder. (Or hold Cmd + Shift + N.)
  3. A new folder appears called "untitled folder," with the name highlighted.
  4. Type test and press return. The folder is now called test.
  5. Double-click test to open it. It's empty. That's fine.

Take a screenshot

  1. Hold Cmd + Shift + 4 at the same time.
  2. Your cursor turns into a small crosshair.
  3. Click and drag a rectangle around any part of the screen. When you let go, the screenshot is saved.
  4. By default, the screenshot saves to your Desktop. Look there, you'll see it.

Rename the screenshot

By default, screenshots are named something like Screenshot 2026-08-19 at 3.21.45 PM.png: long, full of spaces, full of capital letters. Let's rename it to follow the conventions we'll use all semester.

  1. Click the screenshot icon on your Desktop once to select it.
  2. Press return. The name becomes editable.
  3. Type my-screenshot and press return again.

Notice: lowercase, hyphen instead of space, no special characters. That's the convention.

Move the screenshot into the folder

  1. Click and hold the screenshot icon on your Desktop.
  2. Drag it on top of the test folder.
  3. Release. The screenshot disappears from the Desktop and is now inside the folder.
  4. Double-click test to confirm it's in there.

Throw the folder away

  1. Click the test folder once to select it.
  2. Press Cmd + delete. The folder moves to the Trash.

You just used Finder. Everything else builds on that.

Show file extensions

A file extension is the part of a file name after the dot, like .wav, .mp3, .png. By default the Mac hides extensions, which is bad for audio work. Turn them on:

  1. Make sure Finder is the active app (click any Finder window).
  2. From the menu bar, click Finder, then Settings.
  3. Click the Advanced tab.
  4. Check the box that says Show all filename extensions.
  5. Close the Settings window.

Part 2 · Set up your folders

In this class, you'll keep two copies of your work: a local copy on the computer you're using, and a master copy on the NAS (the lab's file server). The local copy is where you actually work during a session. The NAS is how your work moves between computers: when you sit at a different lab machine next session, you'll download your work from the NAS to that machine.

Today, since this is your first session, we'll create the local folder, then connect to the NAS to confirm your account works.

Make your local folder

  1. Open Finder.
  2. In the left sidebar, click Documents.
  3. Press Cmd + Shift + N to make a new folder.
  4. Name it lastname using your actual last name, lowercase. Press return.
  5. Double-click the folder to open it.
  6. Press Cmd + Shift + N again to make another new folder. Name it week-01.

This is where today's work will go.

Connect to the NAS

The NAS is the lab's shared file server. Think of it as a giant hard drive that every computer in the room can connect to. Your work goes there at the end of every session.

  1. Click any empty area of the Desktop, or click a Finder window, so Finder is the active app.
  2. Hold Cmd + K. A small window opens that says "Connect to Server."
  3. Type the server address: your TA will write it on the whiteboard.
  4. Click Connect.
  5. Enter the username and password your TA gives you.
  6. Click Connect.

The NAS now appears in Finder's sidebar on the left, under Locations.

Find your folder on the NAS

  1. Click the NAS in Finder's sidebar.
  2. Open the music folder.
  3. Open the students folder.
  4. Find the folder with your last name. That one is yours.
  5. Open it. It should be empty, that's expected. You'll upload your first work to it at the end of class today.
If something goes wrong

The most common problem is a typo in the server address, or a wrong password. Re-read what's on the whiteboard, retype carefully, and try again. If you still can't connect, raise your hand. Your TA will help.

Eject the NAS for now

You don't need the NAS connected during the session. You'll reconnect at the end of class to upload your work. For now, eject:

  1. In Finder's sidebar, find the NAS under Locations.
  2. Click the small eject icon (⏏) next to its name.

Part 3 · File and folder names

This part is short but important. Bad file names cause real problems later. Follow these rules from Day 1.

The rules

  • Lowercase only. No capital letters.
  • Hyphens, not spaces. Use my-project, not My Project.
  • No special characters. No &, !, @, #, $, %, (, ).
  • Be descriptive. A good name tells you what's inside without opening it.
  • Use versions. When you make changes, save as v1, then v2. Don't overwrite.

The format for this class

lastname-projectname-version.ext

Examples:

thiebaut-hello.m4a
smith-soundpiece-v1.wav
garcia-fieldrec-traffic.wav
Why these rules

Capital letters, spaces, and special characters get treated inconsistently by different software and operating systems. A file named My Project (final!).wav will eventually break something, even if it works on your machine right now. The rules above are the convention used in audio production studios for the same reason.

Part 4 · Set up your gear and make a recording

This is the longest part of today, and the most important. By the end of it, you'll have your gear plugged in, your levels set, and your first audio file saved in your local folder. Take your time. The order of these steps matters: going out of order can mean blasting your ears or recording something that's distorted.

What's at your station

Four pieces of gear:

  • USB hub: the small box with several USB ports. Already connected to the computer (mounted behind the monitor). Don't unplug the hub from anywhere.
  • Audio interface: the box with knobs. Converts analog sound (from a mic, or to your headphones) into digital sound the computer can work with.
  • Microphone: a tabletop mic. Plugged into the audio interface using an XLR cable (the thick three-pin cable).
  • MIDI keyboard: a small piano-style controller. Sends note information to the computer; doesn't make sound on its own.

Step 1 · Turn the interface knobs all the way down

Before plugging anything in or putting headphones on, find these three knobs on your audio interface and turn each one all the way down (counterclockwise, to zero):

  • Gain knob (sometimes labeled "Input" or with a number; there's one per input channel). Controls how much the interface boosts the mic signal coming in.
  • Main / Output knob (sometimes labeled "Monitor" or "Output"). Controls how loud the audio plays back to monitors and headphones.
  • Headphone knob (labeled with a headphones icon). Controls headphone volume specifically.
Why all the way down first

If a cable is loose or something is set incorrectly, you can get a sudden loud burst of sound when you plug in. Starting with everything at zero protects your ears and the gear. You'll always do this before plugging anything in, for the rest of your life as someone who works with audio.

Step 2 · Plug everything in

  1. Plug the audio interface's USB cable into any open port on the USB hub.
  2. Plug the microphone's XLR cable into the input on the front of the audio interface (the round 3-pin socket).
  3. Plug the MIDI keyboard's USB cable into any open port on the USB hub.
  4. Plug your headphones into the headphone jack on the audio interface (labeled with a headphones icon). The jack is on the front or the back of the interface depending on the model, look for the icon.

You can leave the headphones around your neck for now. You won't hear anything in them yet because the gain isn't set: that comes in a few steps.

Step 3 · Slide the headphone volume control all the way up

The headphones in the lab have a small in-line volume slider on the cable itself. Slide it all the way up right now. We'll control your actual volume from the audio interface knob, but if this slider is anywhere below maximum, you'll hear nothing or barely anything no matter what you do, and you'll think the gear is broken.

If you can't hear anything later

Check this slider first. It's the single most common reason people think their headphones aren't working.

Step 4 · Verify the interface is connected

  1. Hold Cmd + Space. A search bar opens in the middle of the screen.
  2. Type Audio MIDI Setup and press return. The app opens.
  3. On the left, you should see your audio interface listed. (It will have the brand name on it, like "Focusrite Scarlett" or "PreSonus AudioBox.")
  4. Right-click your interface in the list, and click Use this device for sound input.
  5. Right-click again, and click Use this device for sound output.
  6. Quit Audio MIDI Setup: from the menu bar at the top, click Audio MIDI Setup, then Quit Audio MIDI Setup. (Or press Cmd + Q.)
Closing vs. quitting on a Mac

On a Mac, clicking the red dot at the top-left of a window only closes the window: the app keeps running in the background. To actually quit an app, use Cmd + Q, or go to the app's menu (the bold name at the top-left of the menu bar) and choose Quit. This matters when working with audio software, because two apps trying to use the same audio interface at once will conflict.

If your interface isn't listed

Unplug the USB cable from the hub, wait a moment, and plug it back into a different port on the hub. If still nothing, raise your hand.

Step 5 · Open QuickTime and start a new audio recording

QuickTime is going to act as our level meter: we'll watch it to see how strong the mic signal is.

  1. Hold Cmd + Space, type QuickTime Player, press return.
  2. QuickTime opens. (If it shows a dialog asking what to open, close it.)
  3. From the menu bar, click File, then New Audio Recording.
  4. A small window appears with a red record button in the middle.
  5. Click the small downward arrow just to the right of the red button.
  6. From the menu, pick your audio interface as the source.

You'll see a horizontal level meter under the record button. Right now it should be flat: we haven't set the gain yet.

Step 6 · Bring up your headphone monitoring

This lets you hear yourself before recording.

  1. Put the headphones on.
  2. Slowly turn up the headphone knob on the interface to about noon (12 o'clock, the knob pointing straight up).
  3. Slowly turn up the main / output knob on the interface to about noon as well.

You probably still won't hear anything yet: that's because the gain isn't set. That's next.

Step 7 · Set the mix knob (only if your interface has one)

About half of the interfaces in the lab have an extra knob labeled Mix or Direct/USB. If yours doesn't have this knob, skip this step: your interface handles the balance automatically.

If yours does have it: this knob controls what you hear in your headphones. On one side is the direct input (the live sound coming from your mic right now). On the other side is the computer playback (sound coming back from QuickTime, Audacity, or whatever software is running).

  1. Find the knob labeled Mix, Direct/USB, or with arrows pointing to two sides.
  2. Set it to about 60% toward the direct/input side, 40% toward the computer/playback side. Roughly the 11 o'clock position if "direct" is on the left.
Why 60/40

When you're recording, you mainly want to hear yourself (the direct input) so you can monitor your performance with no delay. But you also want to hear a little of any playback the computer might be sending (like a click track or backing audio). 60/40 favors your live signal while keeping computer playback audible. We'll revisit this in Module 3.

Step 8 · Set the gain by talking and watching the meter

This is where the signal chain comes alive.

  1. Speak at normal volume into the mic. Keep talking: say what you had for breakfast, count to twenty, anything.
  2. While talking, slowly turn up the gain knob on the interface.
  3. Watch QuickTime's level meter. As you turn up the gain, the meter will start moving with your voice.
  4. Stop turning up when the meter is regularly moving but never hitting all the way to the right. Roughly half to two-thirds across is a good target.
  5. You should also be hearing yourself in the headphones now. If it's too loud or too quiet, adjust the headphone knob.
What you're doing

You just did gain staging: setting the input level so the signal is strong enough to be useful, but not so strong it distorts. We'll come back to this properly in Module 3 when we focus on recording. For today, the rule is: meter moving = good, meter pinned all the way to the right = too hot, turn down.

Step 9 · Record

  1. Click the red record button.
  2. Say your name and one word about why you're taking this class.
  3. Click the same button (now a stop button) to stop.
  4. Click the small play button to listen back. If you hear yourself clearly, it worked.

Step 10 · Save the file to your local folder

  1. From the menu bar, click File, then Save.
  2. A save dialog opens.
  3. In the Name field, type lastname-hello (using your actual last name, no caps, no spaces).
  4. In the sidebar of the save dialog, click Documents.
  5. Navigate into your lastname folder, then into week-01.
  6. Click Save.

The file is now saved locally. You touched the entire audio signal chain to get here: mic, cable, interface, gain, software, file. That's the foundation for everything we'll do this semester.

Before you leave today · the exit routine

Lab machines are shared. You worked locally during the session; before you leave, you'll upload your local folder to the NAS so you can access it from any computer next time. This is the same routine you'll do every session for the rest of the semester.

  1. Make sure your work is saved (Cmd + S in whatever app you used).
  2. Connect to the NAS: Cmd + K, enter the address, sign in.
  3. Open two Finder windows side by side: one on your lastname/ folder on the NAS, one on ~/Documents/lastname/.
  4. Drag your local lastname/ folder onto the NAS to upload it. If macOS asks because a folder with that name already exists, choose Replace (not Keep Both, which creates duplicates).
  5. Eject the NAS from Finder's sidebar.
  6. Sign out of Canvas and any other browser accounts. Quit your browser (Cmd + Q).
  7. Quit all other apps with Cmd + Q.
  8. Audio interface knobs back to zero. Headphones away. Chair in.

The full version of this routine (including what to do at the start of every session, when you sit down at a computer) is in Handout 02 · Session Routines, which is also printed and posted at every station.
