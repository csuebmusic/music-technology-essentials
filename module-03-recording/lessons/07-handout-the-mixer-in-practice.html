The Mixer in Practice

Monday's reading walked the console section by section: channel strip, in-line architecture, submasters, master. Today you watch it work. The pedagogical move is to take the same band, with the same instruments and the same set of mics, and run them through the console twice: once for a live show in a small venue, once for a studio recording session. The hardware doesn't change between the two; only what's plugged into the inputs and where the outputs go.

1 · Meet the band: The Sound Below

The Sound Below is a five-piece. The instrumentation was chosen to exercise every signal type, mic kind, and cable you've met in this module: a mic-level vocal, a mic-level guitar amp, the canonical DI-dry-plus-amp-wet bass treatment from Lecture 2, the standard four-mic drum setup (kick, snare, and a stereo pair of overhead condensers), and a stage keyboardist with a laptop running alongside (the only line-level signals in the band). Twelve signals total. The console has sixteen input channels; the band fills three-quarters of it.

Why the bass gets two channels

Bass is the one source on the band today that gets recorded two ways at once. The bassist's signal hits a DI box first: the box's direct out sends a clean, balanced copy straight to the console (the "dry" signal), and the box's thru jack passes the instrument signal along to the bass amp, where a dynamic mic on the cab captures the amp's tone (the "wet" signal). Two channels, two mic-level XLRs into the console, both arriving at the same moment. At mixdown the engineer blends them: the DI carries the low-end punch and the note attack; the mic'd amp carries the warmth, the room, the speaker's character. Going DI-only often sounds sterile; going amp-only often sounds muddy; blending the two is the standard solution.

Why the keyboardist's signals look different

The keyboardist's four signals are the first non-microphone sources on the band today, and the first line-level signals you've routed into the console. The stage keyboard puts out a stereo pair from its main outputs (L and R, 1/4" TRS, line level); the laptop puts out a second stereo pair from its audio interface (also L and R, 1/4" TRS, line level). Four line-level signals total.

These plug into the LINE jacks on the back of the console, not the MIC jacks. The reason: line-level signals are already strong enough to pass through the console's circuitry without needing the preamp's amplification. The mic preamp on each channel exists to boost weak mic-level signals up to the level the rest of the console expects; sending a line-level signal into a mic preamp would over-amplify it and clip. On each input strip's front panel, the LINE button (top of the strip) switches the channel between its rear-panel MIC and LINE jacks. For the keys and laptop channels, that button gets pushed in.

2 · Scenario 1 · Live sound

The Sound Below is playing a small venue. The stage holds the band, the amps, the drums, the keys rig, and five monitor wedges (one in front of each performer). Out in front of the stage are the front-of-house speakers facing the audience. Halfway back in the room sits the engineer, behind the console.

The job: take the twelve signals coming off the stage and send them to two destinations at once.

  1. Front of house (FOH): the speakers facing the audience. The audience needs to hear the whole band balanced as a mix. This is the L-R master output.
  2. Stage monitors (wedges): a separate mix for each performer, so each player hears what they need to perform well. The vocalist mostly wants their own voice plus a bit of the kick to lock the timing; the bassist wants kick, snare, and their own bass; the drummer wants the bass and the click; the guitarist wants vocal, guitar, and drums; the keyboardist wants vocal, her own keys and laptop, and enough drums to lock in. Five performers, five different mixes, all built from the same twelve channels.

The FOH mix: routing to L-R

Each channel's L-R button sends that channel to the master stereo bus. The channel pan knob places it left or right; the channel fader sets its level in the mix; the channel EQ shapes its tone. The engineer rides faders, makes EQ moves, and uses the master fader to set the overall level coming out of the FOH speakers.

For drums, the standard move is the one the reading walked through: route the four drum channels (5, 6, 7, 8) to subgroup pair 1-2 instead of straight to L-R. Pan the kick and snare center; pan overhead L hard left and overhead R hard right. Then route subs 1 and 2 to the L-R master with their own pair of faders. Now the engineer has one pair of submaster faders that controls the whole drum kit's level in the FOH mix, without touching the individual drum-mic balance. If the drums need to come down 3 dB during a quiet verse, two faders move; the kit's internal balance stays exactly where it was.

The same logic applies to the keyboardist's four channels. Route the keys L/R and laptop L/R (channels 9 through 12) to subgroup pair 3-4. Pan each L channel hard left and each R channel hard right; the two stereo pairs sum across the subgroup pair into one stereo electronic submix. Subs 3 and 4 then route to L-R with their own fader pair. One pair of faders controls everything coming from the keys rig in the FOH mix, and the internal balance between the stage keyboard and the laptop stays set wherever the keyboardist's gain-staging put it.

The wedge mixes: building five different mixes on the aux sends

The five performers each need a different blend in their wedge. The aux sends are how that gets built. Each aux is its own bus, with its own master level, going out the back of the console to a separate output. Send that output to a wedge amplifier, and the aux's bus becomes that performer's monitor mix.

For monitor wedges, every aux used for a wedge mix is set pre-fader. The reason: the FOH engineer might pull the vocal fader down between songs (chatter, a quiet intro, anything), and the singer still needs to hear their own voice in the wedge at the same level. Pre-fader means the aux send level is independent of the channel fader; whatever the engineer does at FOH doesn't bleed into the stage mix.

The console has six aux sends per channel. Five performers take five auxes (1 through 5) for their wedges. That leaves Aux 6 for a single shared FX send to the outboard reverb unit; the delay drops out of this evening's setup. A larger console with more auxes wouldn't force the trade-off, but on the Toft, with five wedges to feed, the engineer chooses what matters most for the show.

Aux 6 is doing a different job from the five wedges: it feeds an outboard reverb unit instead of a monitor speaker. It runs post-fader because the wet effect should follow the dry signal in the FOH mix. If the vocal fader comes down during a quiet section, the reverb on the vocal should come down with it; setting Aux 6 post-fader makes that happen automatically. The reverb return comes back into a spare submaster strip on the FX returns section (where the reading covered the stereo effects returns), where it gets blended into the L-R master alongside the dry signals.

One source, seven destinations

The vocalist's mic on Ch 1 ends up in seven places at once during a live show: the L-R master (audience hears it), the vocal wedge (singer hears it), the four other wedges (everyone else hears it in their stage mixes), and the reverb send (so vocals carry a tail). Seven destinations, one mic, one channel strip. The aux sends are what make that possible; the river-with-taps diagram from Monday's reading is exactly this picture at work.

3 · Scenario 2 · Studio recording session

Same band. Same twelve signals. Same twelve channels on the console. But now the venue is gone: the band is in a studio's live room, the engineer is in the control room behind the glass, and the goal isn't a real-time mix for an audience; it's a clean multitrack recording that can be mixed later.

Three things shift. First, the L-R master no longer feeds speakers in the room with the band; it feeds the control-room monitors (the speakers the engineer is listening to). Second, every channel needs to land on its own track in the DAW so it can be edited independently later. Third, the audience is replaced by a set of headphones on each player's head; the wedge mixes become headphone cues, built the same way (aux sends) but going to a different physical destination.

Getting every channel to the DAW

The studio uses an audio interface as the bridge from the console's analog signals to the computer's digital tracks. Each input channel on the console has a DIR. O/P jack on the back (Lecture 3, section 2): a tap of that channel's post-fader signal, sent straight out to a single destination independently of the routing buttons. Patch Ch 1's direct out into input 1 on the audio interface, Ch 2's direct out into input 2, and so on for all twelve channels. The DAW gets twelve independent tracks; each track is exactly what was on its channel strip, with the engineer's input gain and channel EQ already applied.

Two ways to get tracks to the DAW

Direct outs aren't the only option for getting channels to a multitrack recorder. The alternative is to route channels to subgroups and then send the subgroup outputs to the DAW. The reading's drum example is a setup for this: route the four drum mics to subgroup pair 1-2, and the DAW gets one stereo drum track that has the kit pre-mixed at the console. The trade-off: simpler at mixdown (one stereo track instead of four), but you can't change the balance among the drum mics afterward.

The everyday studio choice is to send everything to the DAW via direct outs (one track per mic, total flexibility at mixdown), and use subgroups only for the engineer's live control-room mix during tracking. That way the DAW captures the most flexible version, and the subgroups serve the engineer's ears in the moment.

The headphone cues: same aux sends, different destination

The performers in the live room need to hear themselves and each other while they play; without headphones, the only thing in the room is acoustic bleed from drums and amps. Each performer gets a pair of headphones connected to a cue mix: a personalized blend of the channels, built on the aux sends in exactly the same way as the wedges in Scenario 1.

The vocalist's cue is built on Aux 1, the guitarist's on Aux 2, the bassist's on Aux 3, the drummer's on Aux 4, the keyboardist's on Aux 5. Each aux runs out of the console to a headphone amplifier (a small box that distributes the cue signal to multiple headphone jacks at performer-comfortable levels). All five auxes stay pre-fader, for the same reason as live: the engineer's level moves on the channel faders shouldn't bleed into what the performers are hearing.

What's a click track

A click track is a metronome the players hear in their headphones while they record. Each beat is a short click sound at the song's tempo; the players follow it so the recording lands on a steady tempo grid. Useful when the song will be edited later (clicks make it easy to line up takes), or when there's no drummer carrying the time, or when the band wants to record one player at a time (overdubbing) and needs a tempo reference all the takes share. The click usually lives on its own DAW track that gets fed back into the console via a spare input or a tape return; from there it gets sent to whichever aux cues need it.

What the engineer hears: the control-room mix

While the band tracks, the engineer is in the control room listening through the studio monitors. The L-R master output feeds those monitors (via the MASTER O/P jacks and the monitor circuit you met in Lecture 3, section 5). The engineer adjusts faders, EQ, and pans to get a balanced reference mix for their own ears. None of this affects what gets recorded: the DAW is capturing each channel's direct out independently, so the engineer's mix decisions during tracking are just for monitoring; the real mix happens later, in front of the DAW.

At mixdown: the I/P REV move

The band leaves; the engineer comes back the next day to mix. The twelve tracks are now sitting in the DAW. To mix on the console, the engineer needs each DAW track playing back through its own channel strip, with the big fader, the four-band EQ, and the routing all available for shaping. The way to do that on an in-line console is the I/P REV button.

The DAW's playback outputs are wired to the console's MON jacks on the back: DAW output 1 to Ch 1's MON jack, DAW output 2 to Ch 2's MON jack, and so on. Normally (during tracking) the MON input feeds the small monitor knob in the middle of the strip. When the engineer presses I/P REV at the top of every input strip, the two paths swap: the signal coming in through MON now flows through the big channel fader with the full EQ and aux-send chain. The mic input (which has nothing on it now anyway, since the mics have gone home) flows through the smaller monitor path, where it's silent. Twelve presses of I/P REV, and the twelve DAW tracks are sitting on the twelve channel strips with full processing available.

Why the in-line design pays off here

On a non-in-line console (called a split design), the channels and the monitor returns live on entirely separate sets of strips, and a typical twelve-channel tracking session would mean the engineer is mixing on a different set of faders from the one they tracked through. On an in-line console, the channel and its monitor return share the same strip, and the I/P REV button is the swap-over. One row of faders carries the band live; the same row, with the button pressed, carries the band at mixdown. The mixing fingers stay on the same strip.

The shape of the work is the same: take many signals in, shape each one, build the mixes the situation calls for, send the result somewhere. The hardware is identical. The patching, and the destination of every output, is what changes between contexts.

The same observation extends past these two scenarios. A film mixing stage, a broadcast truck, a live-streamed church service, an album-mixing session, a podcast studio: the console at the center is doing the same operations. Channels, EQ, fader, pan, routing to a subgroup or a master, aux sends to wherever the situation needs parallel mixes. Once the underlying operations make sense, every new context is a re-patching exercise rather than a re-learning exercise.
