Recording on Your Phone

How to capture sounds on your phone in a format we can use later.

Your library doesn't have to stop at the lab door. With the right app on your phone and a few settings set once, you can record anything that catches your ear: a kettle boiling, a bike chain ticking, footsteps in a stairwell, a friend's voice, a door that sticks. This card walks through the app to install, the settings to set, and the recording technique that separates a usable take from an unusable one. Getting the recordings off the phone and into Audacity is its own short workflow, covered on a separate card.

1 · The app for your phone

The course recommends two apps, one for each platform. Both are built specifically for field recording, both export WAV files Audacity opens directly, and both give you actual control over how the recording is made instead of applying automatic "make it sound good" processing.

iPhone

Lossless Field Recorder by edson engineering. Free on the App Store: apps.apple.com/us/app/lossless-field-recorder/id1644208446. Requires iOS 15 or later.

Android

Field Recorder by Pfitzinger Voice Design. $5.95 on Google Play: play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=de.pfitzinger.rec. Single-developer app, maintained for over a decade, built around the idea that you should control what your microphone captures rather than letting the manufacturer's defaults decide for you.

Install whichever one matches your phone, accept the microphone permission when the app asks, and move to setup.

2 · Setup, by platform

iPhone (Lossless Field Recorder)

The app has no separate Settings screen. Tap the microphone icon on the main screen to open the three choices that shape every recording: mic mode, frequency mode, and pickup pattern.

Mic mode picks which of the iPhone's three built-in microphones to record from. Modern iPhones have a bottom mic (next to the charging port), a front mic (in the notch or Dynamic Island at the top of the screen), and a back mic (next to the rear cameras). Each one faces a different direction:

  • Bottom is the iPhone's main mic, the same one used for phone calls. It's the most predictable and the cleanest signal for general sound capture. Start here when you're not sure.
  • Front faces the same direction as the screen, so it picks up whatever's in front of you when you're looking at the phone. Good for sounds you're examining up close, or when the phone is sitting screen-up.
  • Back faces the same direction as the rear cameras. Good when you can point the camera at the source: a faucet across the kitchen, a door opening, a person on the other side of a table. Also good for surface sounds: lay the phone face-down on the surface itself, the back mic is then pointing right at it.

Frequency mode sets the sample rate. Choose 48 kHz. It's the standard rate for digital audio recording and the native rate for most phone hardware. (96 kHz is technically higher resolution but produces much larger files for no audible benefit on a phone mic.) The app records at 24-bit depth automatically; you don't pick it. So every recording you make is 48 kHz / 24-bit WAV. This is different from the Module 2 Audacity project standard of 44.1 kHz / 16-bit, which means imported phone recordings need a sample-rate conversion (resampling) before they fit into the library. Wed Wk 7's lab walks through that step. Resampling is a normal, routine operation in recording work; it's not a sign anything went wrong.

Pickup pattern sets how the mic responds to direction. The available options depend on which mic mode you picked:

  • With bottom, only omni is available. The bottom mic is a single physical microphone with no directional shaping.
  • With front, you get omni, cardioid, or stereo. Omni picks up from all directions equally. Cardioid focuses on what's in front of the mic and rejects sound from behind. Stereo combines multiple iPhone mics to create a left/right image.
  • With back, you get omni, subcardioid, or stereo. Subcardioid is like cardioid but less directional, picking up some sound from the sides.
Polar patterns, a quick recap

Monday's lecture introduced cardioid as one pattern out of a larger family. The image below shows the most common ones. Each shape is the mic's sensitivity to sound coming from different directions: the front of the mic is at the top of each shape, and the further the shape extends in any direction, the more strongly the mic picks up sound from there.

The iPhone app's pickup-pattern menu names match these shapes: omni is the sphere, cardioid is the heart, subcardioid sits between omni and cardioid. Stereo isn't on this chart; it's the app combining multiple iPhone mics to produce a left-right image rather than a single pattern.

For most sounds, start with omni. Omni is the most forgiving and the most honest: it captures what the mic actually hears. Try cardioid or subcardioid when you want to reject room noise or other sounds coming from behind the mic. Try stereo for ambient recordings where left/right separation matters (a hallway, a courtyard, anywhere the spatial feel is part of the recording).

Android (Field Recorder)

Two things to set:

Input configuration. The switch labeled In:MicA at the top of the display cycles through several input configurations: In:MicA through In:MicE, plus In:Raw. These don't just pick a microphone; they pick how Android pre-processes the signal before sending it to the app. Most configurations apply manufacturer-chosen processing (automatic gain control, noise suppression, echo cancellation). In:Raw turns that pre-processing off and gives you the rawest possible signal, which is what you want.

However, In:Raw sometimes disables stereo on certain phones, and on a few phones it doesn't work at all. The practical first step is to check the Factory menu (tap "Factory" in the display) for a preset uploaded by someone with your phone model: cloud presets are listed by manufacturer and model, and a matching one will already have the right input configuration figured out. If no matching preset exists, set the input to In:Raw and test it by tapping the pause button to enter record-ready mode and watching the level meters respond as you tap near the phone. If the meters work, you're good. If they don't, cycle through In:MicA to In:MicE until you find one that does.

Sample rate and bit depth. Open Settings → Rec. Set sample rate to 48 kHz (the standard digital rate). Turn on 24 bit file write if your phone supports it (Android 6 and later do). This gives you 48 kHz / 24-bit WAV files, which matches what the iPhone app produces.

Once you have a configuration that works, save it as a preset: tap the preset name in the display, then Save, and give it a name you'll recognize. Field Recorder loads your last-used preset on every restart, so this is a one-time setup.

3 · The take

A short list of habits that separate a recording you can use from one you can't.

Airplane mode on

Two reasons. First, a text message or phone call mid-take ruins the recording. Second, on some phones the radio signals from cellular and Wi-Fi can leak into the audio hardware as a faint clicking or buzzing. Airplane mode kills both problems at once. Toggle it on before you start, off when you're done.

Set the phone down

Your hand on the phone produces handling noise louder than most of the sounds you'll want to record. Place the phone on a stable surface, prop it against something, or wedge it between two objects. If you have to hold it, hold the case as still as you can and keep your fingers away from the mic openings.

Distance shapes the recording

Close (a few inches from the source) captures fine detail and lets the sound dominate over the room. Far (a few feet) captures the room around the source, with the source as one element inside it. Neither is right or wrong; they're different recordings of the same event. When you're not sure, take both and pick later.

Watch the level meter

Both apps show a real-time level meter while you're in record-ready mode and during recording. Watch the peak indicator. If it consistently hits the top of the meter (or a red clip indicator lights up), the signal is too loud and the recording will distort. Move the phone farther from the source. You want the peaks landing somewhere in the upper third of the meter, not pinned at the top: room to make decisions later, not a recording locked at the loudest the mic can produce.

Wind kills outdoor recordings

Phone mics have no wind protection. If there's any breeze, you'll get a low rumbling whoosh that drowns out everything else. The cheap fix: cup your hand loosely around the mic opening to break the airflow. The better fix: record from a position that puts a wall, your body, or a piece of furniture between the wind and the phone.

4 · What's next

You can grow your library between now and the Wed Wk 7 lab by recording anything that catches your ear and keeping them in the app (we will work on transferring them over to Audacity next week).

Some things that record well on a phone, to get you started:

  • Small mechanical sounds: zippers, latches, hinges, switches, keys, locks
  • Domestic sounds: a kettle, water running, ice in a glass, a fridge cycling, a fan
  • Object-on-object sounds: pebbles in a jar, a wooden spoon on a pot, a coin on a table
  • Body sounds: footsteps on different surfaces, a hand brushing fabric, a clap in a stairwell
  • Found environments: an elevator, a hallway, a courtyard at dusk, a parking garage

The point of the library is to give you raw material to work with later. A sound that seems boring in the moment may turn out to be exactly what a piece needs three weeks from now. Record more than you think you'll use.
