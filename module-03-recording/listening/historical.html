Listening: Three ways to mix a record

The same handful of sounds, mixed three different ways. Listen for how present the mixing is.

In the mixer sessions you saw a console route many signals at once: a live mix for an audience, a studio mix for recording. Routing and level are choices, and a finished record is the sum of thousands of them. This listening is about those choices. The three pieces below sit at three points on a single spectrum, running from a mix that works to disappear to a mix that becomes the music itself.

Before you listen: a spectrum of mixing

Take three sounds, say a voice, a bass, and a drum kit. Hand them to three different engineers and you can get three records that share almost nothing. The difference is not the sounds. It is what the mixing decides to do with them.

At one end of the spectrum, the engineer works to disappear. The goal is to capture a real performance in a real room as faithfully as possible: the players, the space between them, the air around them. A few well-placed mics, light intervention, and the recording stands in for being in the room. You judge this kind of mixing by how little you notice it.

In the middle, the studio builds something that never happened in any single room. Parts are recorded separately and stacked, instrument on instrument, until they fuse into one wall of sound. Phil Spector called his results "little symphonies for the kids." No group ever performed the finished record live; it exists only as a recording. The mixing is doing an enormous amount of work, and that work goes toward making the construction sound like one convincing whole.

At the far end, the mixing is the composition. In early-1970s Jamaica, engineers like King Tubby and Lee "Scratch" Perry took finished multitrack tapes and reworked them live at the desk: dropping the vocal out entirely, pushing the drums and bass to the front, sending a single snare hit into a long echo, sweeping a filter until a part dissolved. Before them, a mixing desk balanced levels toward a clean final mix. They played the desk like an instrument, and the engineer stepped to the front of the record instead of standing behind it. This is the birth of dub, and it is the reason a producer today reaches for delay and reverb as creative tools, not just corrections.

Knowing where a record sits on this spectrum tells you what to listen for. In the first, listen past the music to the room. In the second, try to count the layers, and notice that they could never share one stage. In the third, follow the engineer's hands. As you listen, hold onto one fact: you are now the person making these decisions. Every sample you record, and every choice you make about it, lands somewhere on this same line.

Listen to all three

1 · Capture the room · Bill Evans Trio, Gloria's Step (1961)

Recorded live at the Village Vanguard, a small New York jazz club, on a single Sunday in June 1961: piano, bass, and drums, three players listening hard to each other. The engineer's job here is to vanish. Listen for the size of the room, where each instrument sits in the space, and the sound of an audience a few feet away. Almost nothing stands between the performance and you.

▶ Listen on YouTube: Bill Evans Trio, Gloria's Step (live, 1961) ~6 min · opens in a new tab ↗

2 · Build the record · The Ronettes, Be My Baby (1963)

Phil Spector's most famous production, cut at Gold Star Studios in Hollywood. Spector recorded a small orchestra of session players and layered them, drenched in echo, into a single massive sound. He built it instrument by instrument, with the drums added last. Listen for how dense and seamless it is: a sound far too large and too reverberant to have come from any one room, presented as one record.

▶ Listen on YouTube: The Ronettes, Be My Baby (1963) ~3 min · opens in a new tab ↗

3 · Play the console · Augustus Pablo & King Tubby, King Tubby Meets Rockers Uptown (1976)

A dub version of a reggae song, "Baby I Love You So." King Tubby took the finished multitrack tape and remixed it live at his desk, turning the song into something new. The vocal is mostly gone. Drums and bass carry everything. Sounds drop out and crash back in, and single hits trail off into long echoes. Listen for the engineer making moves in real time: this is the mix as a performance.

Questions

Answer all four. Listen to each piece at least once before you write.

  1. What space do you hear? For each of the three, describe the sense of space. Where do the instruments seem to be, how much room and echo is around them, and how "live" or "constructed" does the whole thing feel?
  2. Where is the engineer? For each piece, is the mixing trying to disappear, build something whole, or step to the front? Point to one specific thing you hear that places it on the spectrum.
  3. The console as instrument. In the dub track, name two specific moves the engineer makes that you do not hear in the Bill Evans or the Ronettes recording (for example, a dropout, an echo on a single hit, a sudden filter sweep). Describe what each one does to the track.
  4. Your own ear. When you record your own samples, which approach pulls at you: capturing a sound as it truly is in the room, or shaping it into something new? There is no right answer. Notice your instinct, and say why, in a sentence or two.

A note on listening

Use headphones if you can, and listen to each piece all the way through once before you start writing. These three records were made decades apart, in different countries, for different audiences. What connects them is a decision every engineer makes: how much the mix should show. Listen for that decision, and write what you actually hear.

Why this listening, in this module

You just learned what a mixer does. This is where you hear what it is for. The same desk that handles a live show or a studio session can also become the instrument that makes the record. Keep that range in mind when you start mixing in Module 4.
